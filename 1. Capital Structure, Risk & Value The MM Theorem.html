1. Perfect Capital Markets — Underlying Assumptions

Perfect capital markets form the foundational assumption set of the MM theorem.

  • Same Set of Securities at Competitive Prices: Investors and firms can trade the same set of securities at competitive market prices equal to the present value of their future cash flows.
  • No Frictions: There are no taxes, transaction costs, or issuance costs associated with security trading.
  • Financing-Neutrality: A firm's financing decisions do not change the cash flows generated by its investments, nor do they reveal new information about them.

2. Capital Structure Fundamentals

Capital structure is the relative proportions of debt, equity, and other securities a firm has outstanding.

  • All-equity financing — equity is called unlevered equity.
  • Partial debt financing — equity is called levered equity.

3. Unlevered vs Levered Equity — A Numerical Walkthrough

A firm undertakes a risky project with rf = 5%, risk premium = 10%, so rU = 15%.

ScenarioStrongWeakExpected
Project CF1,4009001,150
Unlevered Equity CF1,4009001,150
VU = E[CF] / (1 + rU) = 1,150 / 1.15 = $1,000

If the firm borrows $500 (riskless, rD = 5%), it owes $525 to debtholders:

ScenarioStrongWeakExpected
Debt CF525525525
Levered Equity CF875375625

By the Law of One Price, VL = D + E = $1,000 = VU. With D = 500, levered equity is worth $500.

Law of One Price: Identical investment opportunities trading simultaneously in competitive markets must have the same price, because investors arbitrage rapidly.

Because debtholders are paid first, levered equity is riskier and demands higher returns. The "cheap debt" effect is offset by rising cost of equity, leaving total cost of capital unchanged.

4. MM Proposition I — The Irrelevance Result

In perfect capital markets, the total market value of a firm equals the market value of the cash flows generated by its assets and is independent of its capital structure.

VL = VU = E + D

Reasoning:

  • Without taxes or frictions, all cash flows produced by the assets are distributed to security holders.
  • So total value of all securities = total asset value (Law of One Price).
  • However the pie is sliced between debt and equity, the size of the pie does not change.

Metaphor: Capital structure is like slicing a pizza — no matter how you slice it, the total size remains the same.

5. Homemade Leverage

If investors want a different capital structure than the firm's, they can borrow or lend on their own to achieve their preferred leverage in their portfolios.

Investor PreferenceFirm's StructureInvestor Action
More leverageAll-equityBuy on margin
Less leverageLeveredBuy both D and E

Since investors can replicate any capital structure on their own, the firm's choice does not change the opportunity set, so capital structure does not create value.

6. Market Value Balance Sheet & Leveraged Recapitalization

Market Value Balance Sheet includes all assets and liabilities, including intangibles (reputation, brand, human capital), and uses current market values rather than historical costs. Value is created only by selecting positive-NPV investments.

Leveraged Recapitalization: A firm buys back shares with proceeds from newly issued debt. Share price should not change since both assets and liabilities increase by the same amount.

7. MM Proposition II — Cost of Levered Equity

The cost of capital of levered equity rises linearly with the firm's market-value debt-equity ratio (D/E).

rE = rU + (D/E)(rU − rD)
SymbolMeaning
rU = rAUnlevered cost of equity = cost of assets
rECost of levered equity
rDCost of debt
D/EMarket-value debt-equity ratio

Derivation: rU = (E/(E+D))·rE + (D/(E+D))·rD; solve for rE.

The extra premium (rU − rD) × D/E compensates shareholders for additional financial risk. Even riskless debt raises equity risk.

8. WACC & Capital Structure

WACC = (E/(E+D))·rE + (D/(E+D))·rD = rU = rA

In perfect markets, WACC is independent of capital structure and equals the unlevered cost of capital (the cost of assets).

Intuition: As leverage rises, both debt and equity become riskier and their costs rise; the heavier weight on cheaper debt offsets this exactly. Result: WACC is flat.

In reality, WACC is slightly U-shaped — there's an optimal capital structure that minimizes WACC. Any deviation from MM signals a market friction.

9. Unlevered (Asset) Beta

βU = (E/(E+D))·βE + (D/(E+D))·βD

Asset beta measures the market risk of the firm's underlying assets and can be used to assess the cost of capital for comparable investments.

Net Debt: Cash and risk-free securities reduce asset risk and act as "negative debt." Use net debt = total debt − excess cash/short-term investments.

10. Capital Structure Fallacies

Fallacy 1: Leverage increases EPS. Truth: Leverage raises EPS in good states but lowers it in bad states. The risk of EPS rises proportionally, and shareholders demand higher returns. Effects cancel — share price is unchanged.

Fallacy 2: Equity issuance dilutes shareholders. Truth: As long as new shares are sold at a fair price, cash raised increases assets equally — no gain or loss to existing shareholders.

11. Conservation of Value Principle

In perfect capital markets, financial transactions neither create nor destroy value; they merely repackage risk (and corresponding return). The reverse insight: any observed effect of capital structure on firm value must arise from market frictions (taxes, bankruptcy costs, asymmetric information, agency costs).

12. Debt and Taxes — Interest Tax Shield

Interest expenses are tax-deductible, giving debt a tax advantage.

ITS = τc × Interest Expense
VL = VU + PV(ITS)

For perpetual debt with constant tax rate: PV(ITS) = τc × D.

After-tax cost of debt: rDafter-tax = rD(1 − τc)
After-tax WACC = (E/(E+D))·rE + (D/(E+D))·rD·(1 − τc)

Unlike MM II, with taxes the after-tax WACC declines as debt increases (tax shield grows).

13. Personal Taxes & Effective Tax Advantage

τ* = 1 − [(1 − τc)(1 − τe)] / (1 − τi)
SymbolMeaning
τcCorporate tax rate
τePersonal tax on equity income (dividends + capital gains)
τiPersonal tax on interest income

14. Optimal Capital Structure with Taxes

  • From a pure tax perspective, optimal leverage sets interest expense = EBIT.
  • Because EBIT is uncertain, firms keep interest below expected EBIT for a safety margin.
  • Higher growth rate → higher equity value → lower optimal D/(D+E).
  • Real firms stop short of the tax optimum because debt also brings distress and agency costs.

This leads to the Trade-off Theory: optimal capital structure balances tax shield benefits against financial distress costs.

15. Likely Oral Exam Questions

Q1. State MM Proposition I and its underlying assumptions.
MM I states that in perfect capital markets, the total market value of a firm equals the market value of its asset cash flows and is independent of capital structure (VL = VU). Assumptions: (1) investors and firms trade the same securities at competitive prices, (2) no taxes, transaction or issuance costs, (3) financing is cash-flow and information neutral.
Q2. Why is capital structure irrelevant in perfect markets? Give intuition.
The pizza analogy: cash flows are the pizza; debt and equity are different ways of slicing it. Total size is unchanged. The Law of One Price ensures D + E equals the asset value they replicate.
Q3. Derive the MM Proposition II formula.
Portfolio equivalence: rU = (E/V)·rE + (D/V)·rD. Solving for rE: rE = rU + (D/E)(rU − rD). Cost of equity rises linearly in D/E with slope (rU − rD).
Q4. What is homemade leverage and its role in MM's proof?
Homemade leverage refers to investors replicating any desired leverage in their portfolios via personal borrowing/lending. It is central to the MM I no-arbitrage proof: if VL ≠ VU, investors can construct riskless arbitrage. Hence capital structure does not expand investors' opportunity set or create value.
Q5. Why is "debt is cheaper than equity" misleading?
Although rD < rE, adding debt raises equity risk and rE. The weighted average WACC remains unchanged in perfect markets. The "cheap debt" comes at the cost of "more expensive equity."
Q6. Why doesn't share price change in a leveraged recap?
The firm raises debt and uses proceeds to repurchase shares. Assets first rise (cash from debt) then fall (paid for buyback); liabilities rise by the same debt amount. Both sides of the market-value balance sheet change equally — per-share value of remaining equity is unchanged.
Q7. How does MM change when corporate taxes are introduced?
With corporate taxes, interest is deductible, creating an Interest Tax Shield (ITS = τc × interest). Modified MM I: VL = VU + PV(ITS). For perpetual debt, PV(ITS) = τcD. After-tax WACC decreases with leverage.
Q8. How do personal taxes affect debt's tax advantage?
Investors face different personal tax rates on equity (dividends + capital gains) and debt (interest). Interest is usually taxed at higher ordinary rates while equity income enjoys lower rates, partially offsetting corporate-level debt advantages. Effective τ* = 1 − [(1−τc)(1−τe)] / (1−τi).
Q9. What is the tax-optimal leverage and why don't firms reach it in practice?
Tax-optimal leverage: interest ≈ EBIT (further debt yields no new shield). In practice firms stop short due to (1) EBIT uncertainty requiring a buffer, (2) financial distress and bankruptcy costs, (3) agency costs (asset substitution, underinvestment), (4) asymmetric information. This is the trade-off theory.
Q10. Explain the Conservation of Value Principle.
In perfect markets, financial transactions neither add nor destroy value — they merely repackage risk and return. Any observed effect of capital structure on firm value must arise from market frictions (taxes, bankruptcy costs, asymmetric information, agency costs).
Q11. Does higher EPS imply a higher share price?
Not necessarily. Leverage raises expected EPS when after-tax borrowing cost is below the earnings/price ratio, but it also amplifies EPS volatility, raising required equity returns. In perfect markets these effects cancel — share price unchanged.
Q12. What is asset beta? Why use net debt?
Asset beta (unlevered beta) measures the market risk of a firm's underlying assets — the market-value-weighted average of equity and debt betas. It is independent of capital structure and is useful for cost-of-capital estimation in comparables. Net debt is used because cash and risk-free securities lower overall risk and act as "negative debt," so they're netted out to reflect operating leverage.
Q13. What is the "reverse" value of the MM theorem?
Though MM rests on idealized assumptions, its true contribution is providing a benchmark: any real-world effect of capital structure on firm value must come from market frictions. Subsequent theories — trade-off, pecking order, market timing — extend MM by identifying these frictions one by one.
Q14. When does the after-tax WACC formula break down?
The after-tax WACC formula assumes period tax shield equals D × rD. It breaks down when interest is not fully deductible (e.g., subordinated debt) or when EBIT is insufficient to absorb the full shield. It also assumes equity financing carries no tax shield.
Q15. How does earnings growth rate affect optimal leverage?
Higher growth → higher equity value (PV of future CFs) → lower D/(D+E) at given D. So high-growth firms have lower optimal leverage. Their value comes more from future opportunities than current cash flows, while tax shield benefits depend on current taxable income.
Q16. Why is WACC flat in perfect markets but U-shaped in reality?
In perfect markets, rising rE exactly offsets the lower-cost debt weight, keeping WACC flat. In practice, tax shields lower WACC initially, but at high leverage, financial distress costs, agency costs, and default premiums spike, making rD and rE grow non-linearly. Combined, this yields a U-shape with the minimum at the optimal capital structure.
Q17. What is the market value balance sheet, and why use it?
The market value balance sheet includes intangibles (brand, reputation, human capital) often missing from accounting reports, and uses current market values, not historical costs. It is essential for capital structure analysis because securities are priced at market values and MM results are stated in market terms.
Q18. Why is rU also called rA?
Unlevered equity claims all asset cash flows, so the unlevered cost of equity equals the cost of assets. In MM's perfect markets, rU = rA = WACC, independent of capital structure.
Q19. What real-world frictions cause deviations from MM irrelevance?
Key frictions: (1) Taxes — interest tax shield vs personal taxes; (2) Financial distress and bankruptcy costs — direct (legal/court fees) and indirect (loss of customers, suppliers, staff); (3) Agency conflicts — asset substitution, underinvestment, free cash flow problems; (4) Asymmetric information — leading to pecking order (internal → debt → equity); (5) Market timing — issuing equity when overpriced; (6) Issuance and transaction costs.
Q20. Relationship between MM theorem and modern capital structure theories?
MM is the starting point and benchmark of modern capital structure theory. Modern theories build on relaxing MM assumptions: Trade-off Theory (tax shield vs distress costs), Pecking Order (Myers-Majluf, asymmetric info: internal > debt > equity), Market Timing (issuing when valuations favor), and Agency Theory (Jensen-Meckling, debt disciplines management). All extend MM by introducing specific frictions.

Oral Exam Strategy

  • Define before exemplifying: state each concept in one line, then use analogies (pizza, scale).
  • Always flag assumptions: state "under perfect capital markets" before any MM conclusion.
  • Know the formulas and the intuition: be ready to explain "why" in one sentence.
  • Think in both directions: when asked about MM, extend to real-world deviations (taxes, distress costs, agency).
  • Master the technical terms: homemade leverage, conservation of value, interest tax shield, pecking order, trade-off theory.
  • Connect to cases: e.g., Apple (low leverage + cash) vs highly leveraged airlines.
