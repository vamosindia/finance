1. Return and Risk Fundamentals

Return measures the percentage change in value of an investment per dollar initially invested over a period, allowing comparison across securities.

E[R] = Σ pR × R

Expected return is the average we would earn if we could repeat the investment many times, drawing returns from the same distribution each time.

Var(R) = Σ pR × (R − E[R])2; SD(R) = √Var(R) (volatility)

Variance measures how spread out the distribution of returns is. A risk-free asset has zero variance.

Variance and SD do not differentiate upside vs downside risk. Alternatives: semi-variance (variance of losses only) and expected tail loss (expected loss in worst x% of outcomes).

2. Realized Returns and Estimation Error

Realized return is the return that actually occurs over a particular time period. Common practice: assume future return distribution resembles the past, and use historical average as an estimate of expected return.

  • We cannot observe past investors' true expectations, only realized returns.
  • Average return is only an estimate of true expected return — there is estimation error.
SE = SD(independent observations) / √(number of observations)
95% CI: average return ± 1.96 × SE

Individual stocks are more volatile than portfolios and may have short histories, so estimation error is large. Past average returns are not a reliable expectation for individual stocks.

3. Risk-Return Trade-off

Risk Aversion: Investors require higher expected returns to hold more volatile portfolios.

Risk Premium = average return on investment − average return on risk-free asset (T-bills)

At the portfolio level, higher volatility corresponds to higher average returns. But at the individual stock level, total risk (SD) and average return show no such relationship.

4. Diversification and Two Types of Risk

Firm-specific risk (idiosyncratic, unique, diversifiable): independent risks from firm-specific news; eliminated by holding a large diversified portfolio.

Systematic risk (market risk, undiversifiable): common risks from market-wide news; affect all firms and cannot be diversified.

No-arbitrage argument: a portfolio with only diversifiable risk should earn the risk-free rate. The risk premium for diversifiable risk is zero — investors are compensated only for systematic risk.

Because SD measures total risk (both diversifiable and systematic), SD cannot be used to determine the risk premium of individual stocks. SD remains reasonable for well-diversified portfolios.

5. Measuring Systematic Risk: Beta

An efficient portfolio contains only systematic risk and cannot be diversified further without lowering expected return. The market portfolio (all stocks and securities, e.g., S&P 500) is a natural candidate.

βi = expected % change in security i's (excess) return per 1% change in the market's (excess) return
BetaInterpretation
β = 1Moves with the market
β > 1Cyclical industries, more sensitive to systematic risk
0 < β < 1Non-cyclical, less sensitive
β < 0Negative risk premium; provides insurance against systematic risk

6. Portfolio Return and Risk

E[RP] = Σ wi × E[Ri]
Cov(Ri, Rj) = E[(Ri − E[Ri])(Rj − E[Rj])]
Corr(Ri, Rj) = Cov(Ri, Rj) / (SDi × SDj)
Var(RP) = w12Var1 + w22Var2 + 2w1w2Cov12

Portfolio expected return equals the weighted average return, but portfolio volatility is less than the weighted average volatility. This is the diversification benefit. About 20–30 stocks achieve almost all benefits.

7. Efficient Frontier and Tangent Portfolio

The efficient frontier traces all portfolios with the highest expected return for each level of volatility. Adding more stocks improves the frontier through greater diversification.

E[RP] = (1 − x)rf + x · E[Rrisky]; SD(RP) = x · SD(Rrisky)

When x > 1, the investor borrows at the risk-free rate to invest more in the risky portfolio — known as margin investing.

Sharpe Ratio = (E[RP] − rf) / SD(RP)

The tangent portfolio is where the line from the risk-free asset is tangent to the efficient frontier — it has the highest Sharpe ratio. Every investor holds this tangent portfolio combined with the risk-free asset; only the weights differ based on risk preference.

8. The Capital Asset Pricing Model

Assumptions: No taxes or transaction costs; borrowing/lending at rf; homogeneous expectations; investors hold only efficient portfolios.

Under CAPM, all investors use the same inputs and identify the same tangent portfolio. Market clearing requires tangent portfolio = market portfolio. The line through the risk-free asset and the market portfolio is the Capital Market Line (CML).

E[Ri] = rf + βi × (E[RMkt] − rf)

rf compensates investors for "waiting" (time value of money); the risk premium compensates for "worrying" about investment returns. Investors are not compensated for firm-specific risk.

9. Security Market Line and Alpha

The Security Market Line plots expected return against beta. All fairly priced assets should lie on the SML. Market beta = 1, and the slope is the market risk premium.

PositionInterpretation
On SMLFairly priced (CAPM holds)
Above SMLUnderpriced, positive alpha
Below SMLOverpriced, negative alpha
Ri − rf = αi + βi(RM − rf) + ei

CAPM predicts equilibrium α = 0 for every stock. Positive alpha attracts buyers, raising price and lowering expected return back to fair value; negative alpha causes selling. Rebalancing drives all alphas to zero.

βP = Σ wi × βi

10. Required Return

Required return is the expected return necessary to compensate for the marginal risk that investment i contributes to portfolio P.

ri = rf + βi,P × (E[RP] − rf)

If E[Ri] > required return, adding more of i improves the Sharpe ratio. The final portfolio is efficient when expected return equals required return for every security. When P = market portfolio, this is CAPM.

11. Estimating Equity Cost of Capital

Cost of capital is the best expected return available in the market on investments with similar risk and term.

rE = rf + βE × (E[RMkt] − rf)

Method 1: Historical average excess return. Drawbacks: large standard error even with decades of data; backward-looking, may not reflect current expectations.

Method 2: Fundamental approach. Using the dividend discount model: r = D1/P + g, then subtract rf.

Estimating Beta: Use 2–5 years of weekly or monthly returns; regress excess stock returns on excess market returns; the slope is β. Method assumes β is reasonably stable over time.

12. Cost of Debt

YTM is the IRR an investor earns from holding the bond to maturity with all promised payments. When PD is low, YTM approximates expected return well. When PD is higher, YTM overstates expected return.

rD = YTM − PD × L (where L is expected loss given default)

Alternatively, estimate rD via CAPM using debt beta. Since bank loans and corporate bonds trade infrequently, use betas of bond indices in the same rating category as proxies.

13. Company and Project Cost of Capital

The company cost of capital is the opportunity cost for an investment in all firm assets and is the correct discount rate for average-risk projects.

  • Asset beta + CAPM: estimate unlevered β, then compute cost of capital via CAPM.
  • Weighted average: rU = (E/V)rE + (D/V)rD. More common because cost of debt is observable, while debt β is not.

Projects should be evaluated separately from financing decisions. New projects lack public-trading data, so identify comparable firms in the same line of business and use their betas. If the comparable is all-equity, use its equity beta directly. If levered, first unlever to obtain asset beta before applying it to the project.

14. Unlevered Cost of Capital and After-Tax WACC

Asset cost of capital (unlevered cost of capital) is the expected return required to hold the firm's underlying assets.

rU = (E/(E+D))·rE + (D/(E+D))·rD

Net debt: Cash is a risk-free asset that reduces firm risk and acts as "negative debt." Use enterprise value = equity + net debt, where net debt = total debt − excess cash.

After-tax WACC = (E/V)·rE + (D/V)·rD·(1 − τc)

After-tax WACC is lower than the unlevered cost of capital because it includes a tax adjustment. Interest deductibility means the effective after-tax cost of debt is rD(1 − τc).

TermUseFormula Feature
Unlevered (Pretax) WACCEvaluate all-equity project with same riskUses pretax rD
After-tax WACCEvaluate project with same risk AND same financingUses after-tax rD

The after-tax WACC formula is valid only when the tax shield each period equals D × rD. It breaks down with non-deductible interest or insufficient EBIT, and assumes no tax shield from equity financing.

15. Likely Oral Exam Questions

Q1. Explain expected return, variance, and standard deviation, and their roles in investment analysis.
Expected return E[R] = Σp·R is the average return from repeating the investment. Variance measures the spread; SD is the standard deviation in original units. These form the basis of Markowitz mean-variance analysis but cannot distinguish upside from downside risk; semi-variance or expected tail loss are alternatives.
Q2. Distinguish firm-specific and systematic risk, and explain why investors are only compensated for systematic risk.
Firm-specific risk comes from individual firm events and is independent across firms — it can be diversified away. Systematic risk comes from market-wide factors and affects all firms. The no-arbitrage argument shows a portfolio with only diversifiable risk should earn rf; therefore the premium for diversifiable risk is zero. Investors are compensated only for systematic risk.
Q3. What is beta and why is it the appropriate measure of risk?
Beta is the expected % change in security i's (excess) return per 1% change in the market's (excess) return. It measures only systematic risk; market beta = 1. Beta is appropriate because it is proportional to the marginal risk a security contributes to the optimal risky portfolio. SD includes diversifiable risk, which is not priced.
Q4. Derive and explain the CAPM formula.
Assumptions: no taxes/costs, borrow/lend at rf, homogeneous expectations, investors hold only efficient portfolios. With homogeneous expectations, all investors identify the same tangent portfolio. Market clearing forces tangent = market portfolio. In equilibrium: E[Ri] = rf + βi(E[RM] − rf). rf compensates for waiting; the risk premium compensates for systematic risk.
Q5. What are efficient and tangent portfolios? Why does every investor hold the tangent portfolio?
Efficient portfolios lie on the mean-variance efficient frontier: highest return for given volatility. The tangent portfolio is where the line from rf touches the frontier — it has the highest Sharpe ratio. By the Two-Fund Separation Theorem, every investor optimally combines the tangent portfolio with the risk-free asset. With CAPM's homogeneous expectations, all investors derive the same tangent portfolio, which must equal the market portfolio.
Q6. What is the Sharpe ratio and how is it used in decisions?
Sharpe ratio = (E[RP] − rf) / SD(RP) measures excess return per unit of total risk — the slope of the line from rf through P. Uses: ranking portfolios; identifying the tangent portfolio; evaluating manager performance.
Q7. Explain the SML and alpha.
SML plots (β, E[R]) with intercept rf and slope = market risk premium. CAPM predicts fairly priced assets lie on SML. Alpha = actual E[R] − CAPM-predicted return. Positive alpha = above SML (underpriced); negative alpha = below SML (overpriced). In equilibrium all alphas → 0 through investor rebalancing.
Q8. Distinguish efficient frontier, CML, and SML.
Efficient frontier: optimal portfolios of risky assets in (SD, E[R]) space — a curve. CML: line from rf tangent to frontier at M, in (SD, E[R]) space, describes only efficient portfolios. SML: in (β, E[R]) space, describes equilibrium pricing of all securities. Key difference: CML uses SD (efficient portfolios only), SML uses β (all assets).
Q9. What are CAPM's main assumptions, and what happens if they are relaxed?
Main assumptions: no taxes/costs; borrow/lend at rf; homogeneous expectations; investors hold only efficient portfolios. Relaxations: no risk-free borrowing → Black's zero-beta CAPM; heterogeneous expectations → aggregate relationship may still hold; multiperiod consumption → Merton's ICAPM; empirical anomalies (size, value, momentum) → Fama-French and Carhart multifactor models.
Q10. How do you estimate cost of equity using CAPM?
Steps: (1) rf from Treasury yields matching the horizon; (2) market risk premium from historical excess returns or fundamentals (r = D1/P + g − rf); (3) β from 2–5 years of weekly/monthly returns, regressing stock excess returns on market excess returns; (4) apply rE = rf + βE(E[RM] − rf).
Q11. Why doesn't YTM always equal cost of debt? How is it adjusted?
YTM is the IRR on promised cash flows, assuming no default. When PD is non-trivial, expected cash flows fall short of promised, so YTM overstates expected return. Adjustment: rD ≈ YTM − PD × L, where L is loss given default. Alternative: CAPM with bond-index betas of the same rating.
Q12. Distinguish cost of equity, cost of debt, cost of assets, and WACC.
rE: required return to shareholders, reflects leverage. rD: required return to creditors. rU (asset/unlevered cost): return on underlying business — market-value-weighted average of E and D (= pretax WACC). After-tax WACC: rU reduced by tax shield = (E/V)rE + (D/V)rD(1−τc). In perfect markets rU = WACC; with taxes WACC < rU.
Q13. Why use a project-specific cost of capital rather than company WACC?
Company WACC is appropriate only for projects with average company risk. Using it for higher-risk projects overstates NPV; for lower-risk projects, understates NPV. Use pure-play comparables and their (unlevered) asset betas to estimate project-specific cost of capital.
Q14. Why use net debt instead of total debt for asset beta and WACC?
Cash and short-term investments are risk-free, reducing overall risk and acting as "negative debt." We care about underlying business risk, not cash holdings. Use enterprise value = equity + net debt to accurately reflect operating risk.
Q15. Why does CAPM estimate expected returns more accurately than historical averages?
Historical averages suffer from large estimation error and sample noise dominated by firm-specific events. CAPM concentrates estimation error in one quantity — the market risk premium — which is more precisely estimated using the entire market portfolio. Combined with stable beta estimates, CAPM gives more accurate expected returns.
Q16. Explain the relationship between required return and expected return, and how they determine an efficient portfolio.
Required return compensates for the marginal systematic risk i adds to portfolio P: rireq = rf + βi,P(E[RP] − rf). If E[Ri] > required, increase i to raise Sharpe ratio. The portfolio is efficient iff E[R] = required return for every security. When P = market, this is CAPM.
Q17. When to use pretax vs after-tax WACC?
Pretax WACC (= rU): evaluate all-equity-financed projects (no tax shield). Used in MM analysis and APV. After-tax WACC: evaluate projects with same risk AND same financing as the firm. Used in standard DCF/FCF valuation. With after-tax WACC, do not separately add PV of tax shield — that double counts.
Q18. How does leverage affect equity beta and cost of equity?
Asset beta = (E/V)βE + (D/V)βD reflects pure business risk. Rearranged: βE = βU + (D/E)(βU − βD). Even with risk-free debt, βE grows linearly with D/E. Combining with CAPM gives MM II: rE = rU + (D/E)(rU − rD).
Q19. Why must the tangent portfolio equal the market portfolio under CAPM?
Under homogeneous expectations, all investors derive the same tangent portfolio. Each holds tangent + rf. Market clearing requires aggregate demand = supply (all outstanding securities). So tangent weights must equal market-cap weights. If an asset had zero weight, no one would hold it; its price would drop until its expected return rises enough for inclusion.
Q20. Why is CAPM still useful despite frequent empirical rejection?
Empirical issues: the true "market portfolio" is unobservable (Roll's critique); anomalies (size, value, momentum) suggest one factor is insufficient; beta is unstable. Yet CAPM remains the benchmark: clear risk-return framework; most widely used in practice; multifactor models (Fama-French) extend rather than replace CAPM. Even imperfect, it is more systematic than guessing or pure historical averaging.

Oral Exam Strategy

  • Move from concepts to formulas: explain risk aversion, diversification, systematic risk before introducing beta and CAPM.
  • Always emphasize systematic vs diversifiable: this is the central dichotomy. Risk premium compensates only systematic risk.
  • Distinguish four costs of capital: rE, rD, rU (= pretax WACC), and after-tax WACC.
  • Memorize CAPM assumptions and the consequence of relaxing each.
  • Acknowledge empirical limitations: CAPM is often rejected; multifactor models extend it.
  • Connect to capital structure: when asked about rE, bring in MM II; when asked about WACC, bring in tax shield.
  • Key terms: systematic vs idiosyncratic risk, diversification, efficient frontier, tangent portfolio, Sharpe ratio, capital market line (CML), security market line (SML), alpha, beta, unlevered cost of capital, after-tax WACC.
